1 Introduction

Here, I will present exploratory analysis of data collected as part of a capture-recapture study of snowshoe hares between 1998 and 2009 in the Tanana Valley in Alaska. Juvenile hares were collected from three sites within the study region, classified by sex, and several body measurements were taken. This analysis will focus only on data collected from juvenile hares. I will examine the number of juvenile hares caught through time, differences in body weights between male and female juvenile hares, and allometric relationships between hind foot size and body weight in juvenile hares.

From our Welch two-sample t-test, we estimate the mean male body weight to be 945.8588957 and the mean female body weight to be 855.3908629, for a difference of 90.4680328. The p-value from this t test is 0.0070933, which mean that it is rather unlikely that male and female hares have the same mean body weight. Still, the estimated Cohens d statistic is only 0.2904674, which suggests that the difference in size between males and females is fairly small, even if it is statistically significant.

The plot and the linear model show a positive relationship between snowshoe hare hind foot length and body weight. From the linear model, a slope of 9.5233988 indicates that for every additional 1 mm in hind foot length, a hare will average 9.5233988 g more in body weight. With an r-squared value of 0.2987695, variation in hind foot length explains about 29.9 % of variation in body weight. With a Pearsons r correlation of 0.5465982, there is moderate correlation between these two variables. However, the diagnostic plots indicate some potential violations of linear model assumptions. In particular, the assumption of homoskedasticity might be broken here, as it appears that residuals are generally larger for larger values of hind foot length.

4 Summary

In conclusion, the most juvenile hares were caught early in the study, and fewer were caught in subsequent years. Mean male body weight was larger than mean female body weight, but the difference in these means was small. Body weight appeared positively correlated to hind foot length in juvenile snowshoe hares, though it is not clear that a ordinary-least-squares linear model is appropriate for these data.
